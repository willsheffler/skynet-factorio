# Reinforcement learning primer

**For:** Will (senior protein-design researcher, knows LLMs and diffusion, doesn't know RL)
**Author:** Vellum
**Length:** ~700 words. Calibrated for getting you oriented enough to read FLE's paper and understand skynet-factorio design choices, not for becoming an RL practitioner.

## The one-sentence frame

Reinforcement learning is **gradient-based optimization where the loss
function is replaced by long-horizon reward and the training data is
generated by the agent's own actions.**

Everything else is bookkeeping to make that one sentence work.

## Why it feels like a separate field

Three things make it feel categorically different from the supervised
learning you already understand:

**1. The data distribution shifts during training.** In supervised learning,
your dataset is fixed. In RL, the agent's policy decides what states it
visits, and the dataset is whatever it experienced last episode. As the
policy improves, the data distribution changes. This is the deepest
structural difference.

**2. The reward signal is delayed and sparse.** In supervised learning,
each input has an immediate label. In RL, you might play 10,000 game frames
before seeing the single bit "you won" or "you lost". Attributing reward to
the specific decisions that caused it is the **credit assignment problem**,
and it's the central technical difficulty.

**3. Exploration vs exploitation.** Supervised learning has no analog. The
agent has to balance "do what I think works" (exploitation) against "try
weird things to discover better policies" (exploration). Getting this
balance wrong gives you either a policy that never improves or one that
never converges.

## Connections you already have

**Optimization / solvers**: yes, the RL crowd does talk about RL as
"optimization over policy space" and there's deep formal connections to
control theory, dynamic programming, and constraint solvers. Bellman's
equations are the mathematical backbone — they're basically the
optimality conditions for sequential decision problems, analogous to
KKT conditions for static convex optimization. The connection is real;
RL people are not just flexing when they invoke it.

**LLMs**: you've already met RL. RLHF (the H is "human feedback") is the
final-stage training that turns a pretrained LLM into Claude or GPT-4.
The reward model is trained on human preferences; the LLM is then
fine-tuned via PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization, a standard RL algorithm)
to maximize predicted human-rated reward. So your everyday LLM is an
RL-trained artifact. The novelty in FLE is using LLMs the OTHER way —
LLMs as agents in an RL environment, not LLMs as training targets.

**Diffusion**: less direct. Diffusion is supervised denoising at heart. But
**diffusion policy** (where the action space is generated by a diffusion
model conditioned on observations) is now competitive with PPO on
manipulation tasks. So the two fields are starting to overlap.

## Key algorithms (cheat sheet)

- **Q-learning / DQN**: learn the value of each state-action pair; act
  greedily. Workhorse for discrete action spaces.
- **Policy gradient methods (REINFORCE, PPO, TRPO)**: directly optimize
  the policy via gradient ascent on expected reward. PPO is the modern
  standard for continuous control. RLHF uses PPO.
- **Actor-critic (A2C, A3C, SAC)**: train both a value estimator and a
  policy together; the value estimator reduces variance of policy
  gradients.
- **Model-based RL (Dreamer, MuZero)**: learn an explicit dynamics model
  in addition to a policy. Tends to be much more sample-efficient because
  the model can be "played" without real-environment steps.

## What FLE does

FLE doesn't train an RL policy from scratch. It takes an **already-trained
LLM** (Claude, GPT-4, etc.) and uses it as the **agent** in an RL-style
loop. The LLM's job at each timestep is to read the current game state and
emit a Python program that, when executed, advances the game. The agent
doesn't get gradient updates between episodes — it just gets a longer
context full of "here's what happened last time".

This is called **in-context learning** or **agentic LLM** and is a third
direction beyond classical RL and supervised learning. It's actively
researched; FLE's paper is one of the better evaluation harnesses.

## What skynet-factorio adds

Three layers, roughly increasing in research interest:

1. Scripted heuristics (no learning at all — Phase 0)
2. Local RL policy (classical RL, trained on Factorio — Phase 2)
3. LLM coordinator over RL substrate (hybrid hierarchical — Phase 3)

The third layer is genuinely novel and is what makes this project
academically interesting rather than just a fun toy. If it works it's
publishable; if it doesn't we still have a fun Factorio bot.

## Recommended reading once you want more

- Sutton & Barto, *Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction* (2nd ed) —
  the canonical textbook. First 5 chapters give you the mental model.
  Free PDF on Sutton's website.
- David Silver's RL course lectures on YouTube (10 lectures, DeepMind).
  Still the best video-form treatment.
- FLE paper (arXiv:2503.09617) — short, well-written, gives you the
  agentic-LLM-as-RL-agent framing concretely.

For our project, you don't need to read any of these before Phase 0 ships.
Phase 1 and 2 will give better hooks for when the textbook chapters land.
